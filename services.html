Notarial Acts

Types of Notarizations

Different documents require different notarial acts. The most common are:

Acknowledgment

The signer appears before the notary and confirms they signed the document willingly. The most common notarial act — used for deeds, powers of attorney, and many legal documents.

Jurat

The signer swears or affirms under penalty of perjury that the contents of the document are true. Used for affidavits, sworn statements, and similar documents.

Oath or Affirmation

A verbal commitment to tell the truth or fulfill a duty. Often used in conjunction with jurats or for witnessing verbal statements.

If you're not sure which type of notarization your document requires, check the signature block at the end of the document — it will typically indicate the notarial act needed, or your document preparer can advise you.

Special Locations

Hospital & Care Facility Signings

Some of the most important documents people need notarized — advance health care directives, powers of attorney, and living wills — are often needed when someone is in a hospital, rehabilitation center, or nursing home. I provide on-site notary services at these facilities throughout my service area.

What to Expect

  • I coordinate my arrival with facility visiting hours and access procedures
  • I verify the signer's awareness and capacity before proceeding, as required by California law
  • I handle the appointment with the care and discretion the setting requires
  • All needed witnesses must be present and available — I am not permitted to serve as a witness

Common Documents at These Signings

Good to Know

Important Limitations

In the interest of full transparency, here are some important things to know about what a notary public can and cannot do:

Loan Signings & Mortgage Closings

Loan signings require a separate National Notary Association (NNA) Signing Agent certification. I plan to add this service once I've completed that certification. If you need a loan signing now, I'm happy to refer you.

Legal Advice

A notary public is not an attorney and cannot advise you on what documents to sign, what they mean, or how to fill them out. This is true of all notaries — it is not a service I offer or that any notary can legally provide. If you have questions about the content of your documents, please consult an attorney.
