Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before your appointment

What is a mobile notary?

A mobile notary is a commissioned Notary Public who travels to clients rather than working from a fixed location. Instead of you finding a notary and driving to them, I come to your home, office, hospital, or any other location that works for you — at a time that fits your schedule.

What do I need to bring to the appointment?

The most important thing you need is a current, government-issued photo ID — a driver's license, passport, or state ID card. The ID must not be expired. You'll also need your document ready. Whether to sign it before I arrive depends on the type of notarization — see the question below about signing beforehand. If your document requires witnesses, you'll need to arrange for them to be present; I am not permitted to serve as a witness.

Do I need to sign the document before the appointment?

It depends on the type of notarization. For a jurat (sworn statement or affidavit), you must sign in my presence. For an acknowledgment — the most common type — you may have already signed the document, but you must personally appear before me and acknowledge that the signature is yours and that you signed it willingly. When in doubt, wait to sign until I arrive and I'll guide you through it.

What forms of ID are acceptable?

California law requires a current, government-issued photo ID. Acceptable forms include a California driver's license or ID card, a U.S. passport or passport card, a U.S. military ID, a foreign passport, or a Tribal ID card. The ID must be current (not expired). If you're unsure whether your ID will be accepted, contact me before booking.

What if my document is in a foreign language?

I can still notarize a document in a foreign language, as long as the notarial certificate itself is in English (which I'll attach or complete). However, the signer must be able to communicate with me — either in English or through a translator who is present and is not a party to the document. I cannot translate or interpret documents.

How much does it cost?

Your total depends on your location (zone) and the number of signatures being notarized. California caps the notary fee at $15 per notarized signature. Travel is a flat-rate fee based on zone — $35 for Zone 1 (Glendale, Burbank, La Canada, La Crescenta, Montrose), $50 for Zone 2, and $65 for Zone 3. Additional surcharges apply for after-hours, rush, or hospital/care facility appointments. See the full pricing page for details.

What are your hours?

Standard hours are Monday through Saturday, 9am to 7pm. After-hours appointments (evenings until 9pm and all day Sunday) are available by request and carry a $35 surcharge. Same-day appointments during standard hours are available with at least 2 hours notice, with a $35 rush surcharge.

How far in advance do I need to book?

I can typically accommodate same-day appointments during standard hours with at least 2 hours notice. For the best availability and to avoid the rush surcharge, booking a day or more in advance is ideal. Contact me and I'll do my best to accommodate your timeline.

Can you come to a hospital or nursing home?

Yes. Hospital and care facility signings are a regular part of what I do. I coordinate with facility access requirements and handle these appointments with the care and sensitivity they call for. A $25 facility surcharge applies. Please note that California law requires me to verify that the signer is aware of what they're signing and is acting of their own free will — I take this responsibility seriously.

Do I need witnesses for my document?

Some documents require witnesses in addition to — or instead of — notarization. California Advance Health Care Directives (AHCDs), for example, require either a notary or two adult witnesses. The witness eligibility rules for AHCDs are specific and detailed under California law — certain healthcare providers, facility employees, and others are prohibited from serving as witnesses. I strongly recommend reviewing the witness requirements on your specific form or consulting with the person who prepared it before your appointment. I cannot serve as a witness, and if the wrong witnesses are used, the document may not be legally valid.

Can you certify copies of a birth certificate, death certificate, or marriage license?

No. California notaries are not permitted to certify copies of vital records. For certified copies of birth certificates, death certificates, or marriage licenses, you'll need to contact the issuing agency — typically the county clerk's office or the California Department of Public Health.

Can you help me fill out or explain my documents?

No — and this is an important limitation. A notary public is not an attorney and cannot give legal advice, explain what a document means, tell you whether you should sign it, or help you fill it out. My role is solely to verify your identity and witness your signature. If you have questions about the content of your documents, please consult an attorney.

What happens if I can't be at the location on time?

I allow a 20-minute grace period from the scheduled appointment start time. After that, a waiting fee of $2 per minute applies. If you need to reschedule, please let me know as soon as possible so I can adjust my schedule.

Do you offer loan signing services?

Not yet. Loan signings require a separate National Notary Association Signing Agent certification. I plan to add this service once I've completed that certification. In the meantime, I'm happy to refer you to a qualified loan signing agent.

What payment methods do you accept?

Cash, Zelle, Venmo, and personal checks. Payment is due at the time of service. I do not currently accept credit or debit cards.
